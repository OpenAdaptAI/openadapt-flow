\begin{abstract}
General computer-use agents can operate unfamiliar graphical interfaces, but
repeatedly reasoning through a known workflow introduces latency, inference
cost, and run-to-run variation. Conventional GUI automation avoids repeated
reasoning but can fail under interface drift and often treats a visually
successful action as a successful business effect. We present \system{}, a
demonstration compiler that converts a recorded GUI trace into a deterministic
program. Healthy replay uses structured elements and local visual evidence with
no model calls. When evidence changes, the runtime re-resolves a target,
records a repair for review, or refuses to continue when configured identity,
postcondition, effect, or policy checks cannot verify the action. We describe
the compiler, resolution ladder, governed repair protocol, and system-of-record
effect verification. Bounded experiments include a public OpenEMR workflow, a
CI-reproducible fixture, a 29-application public-web corpus, transactional fault
injection, and adversarial identity tests. The results support the narrower
claim that compiled replay can reduce latency and per-run inference on repeated
workflows; they do not establish long-term enterprise, desktop, RDP, Citrix, or
clinical-production reliability. The release plan publishes the implementation,
reviewed raw artifacts, failure taxonomy, and machine-checked paper constants
with the final paper.
\end{abstract}
